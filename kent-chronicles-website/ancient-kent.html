Ancient Kent

Roman Ruins, Medieval Mysteries & Archaeological Discoveries

The Roman Legacy of Durovernum Cantiacorum

Long before Canterbury became the spiritual heart of England, it was Durovernum Cantiacorum, a thriving Roman settlement that served as the administrative capital of the Cantiaci tribe. Founded around 43 AD following the Roman invasion of Britain, this ancient city represents one of Kent's most significant archaeological treasures, offering invaluable insights into nearly four centuries of Roman occupation.

The Romans chose their location wisely, establishing Durovernum at the crossing of the River Stour where ancient British trackways converged. This strategic position made it a natural hub for trade and administration, connecting the major Roman roads of Watling Street and the coastal route to Dover. Archaeological excavations have revealed the sophisticated urban planning that characterized Roman settlements, including a forum, basilica, temples, and an extensive bath complex.

Archaeological Marvel: The Roman Pavement

Beneath the streets of modern Canterbury lies one of Britain's finest Roman mosaic pavements, discovered in 1868. This intricate artwork, featuring geometric patterns and mythological scenes, demonstrates the wealth and sophistication of Roman Canterbury's elite residents.

Medieval Transformation and Norman Conquest

The transition from Roman rule to Anglo-Saxon settlement marked a profound transformation in Kent's ancient landscape. Following the Roman withdrawal around 410 AD, the region became part of the Kingdom of Kent, one of the earliest Anglo-Saxon kingdoms to emerge in post-Roman Britain. This period saw the establishment of Canterbury as a significant ecclesiastical center, culminating in St. Augustine's mission in 597 AD.

The Norman Conquest of 1066 brought another wave of architectural and cultural change to ancient Kent. The Normans, recognizing the strategic importance of the county's position as the gateway to England, embarked on an ambitious program of castle building and ecclesiastical construction. Dover Castle, begun in the 1180s, represents the pinnacle of medieval military architecture, while Canterbury Cathedral was rebuilt in the Norman style following a devastating fire in 1067.

Archaeological Discoveries and Ancient Settlements

Kent's archaeological landscape continues to yield remarkable discoveries that illuminate the county's ancient past. From prehistoric settlements to Roman villas, medieval churches to abandoned villages, each excavation adds new chapters to our understanding of how people lived, worked, and worshipped in ancient Kent.

Recent archaeological work has revealed the extent of Roman settlement across the county, with major sites at Reculver, Richborough, and Lympne showcasing the sophistication of Roman engineering and urban planning. The discovery of Roman villas at Folkestone and Otford demonstrates the wealth and lifestyle of Romano-British landowners, while excavations at Canterbury have uncovered evidence of continuous occupation from the Iron Age through to the present day.

The Cinque Ports: Medieval Maritime Power

The confederation of the Cinque Ports, including Dover, Sandwich, and Hythe, represented medieval England's most important maritime alliance. These ancient ports provided ships and men for the king's navy in exchange for significant privileges and autonomy, shaping Kent's coastal communities for centuries.

Canterbury Cathedral: Pilgrimage and Ancient Faith

Canterbury Cathedral stands as the most significant monument to ancient Kent's religious heritage, representing over 1,400 years of continuous Christian worship. Built on the site of a Roman church, the cathedral complex encompasses St. Augustine's Abbey and St. Martin's Church, collectively recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site for their outstanding universal value to humanity.

The cathedral's crypt, dating from the 11th century, contains some of the finest examples of Romanesque architecture in England. The carved capitals and columns display the skilled craftsmanship of medieval stonemasons, while the walls bear witness to centuries of pilgrimage following the murder of Thomas Becket in 1170. Archaeological investigations have revealed the complex building history of the cathedral, including Roman foundations, Anglo-Saxon structures, and successive medieval rebuilding campaigns.

Preserving Kent's Ancient Heritage

The work of preserving and interpreting Kent's ancient heritage continues through the dedicated efforts of archaeologists, historians, and heritage organizations. County archaeological services work closely with developers and landowners to ensure that new discoveries are properly investigated and recorded, while museums and heritage sites make these remarkable finds accessible to the public.

Recent technological advances, including ground-penetrating radar and 3D laser scanning, have revolutionized our ability to study ancient sites without damaging them. These tools have revealed hidden structures beneath Canterbury Cathedral, mapped the extent of Roman Durovernum, and identified new archaeological sites across the county. The integration of scientific analysis with traditional archaeological methods continues to enhance our understanding of Kent's ancient past.
